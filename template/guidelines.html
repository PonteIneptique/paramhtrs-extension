Pre-Editorial Normalization (PEN) Guidelines

The text produced by graphemic ATR is raw: it mixes manuscript-specific practices (abbreviations, inconsistent spacing, decorative capitalization, mixed numeral systems, undistinguished u/v and i/j) with recognition errors. The goal of normalization in this tool is to turn that raw output into a text that is usable for scholarship and NLP, while staying as close as possible to what is actually recoverable from the page.

Each guideline below follows the same structure: Rule (the one-sentence decision), Details (how to apply it in the editor), and TEI Equivalent (what it produces in the exported XML).

What to normalize

1. Ramist letters (u/v, i/j)

Rule: Resolve ramist u/v and i/j to their modern consonantal or vocalic value.

Details: Use a Normalization annotation on the full word. The mass-cleanup tool's "surnormalization" check deliberately ignores these substitutions, since they are not over-corrections.

TEI Equivalent: The normalized form appears inline in <w>; the original form is kept in a <span target="#wN"> inside <standOff>.

<choice><orig>uitam</orig><reg>vitam</reg></choice>

2. Spacing and page/column splits

Rule: Only spaces get inserted or deleted as a deliberate normalization choice — never letters.

Details: Insertion applies to a missing word space, or to merging a line-break hyphen into the next line. Deletion is not a normalization choice you make deliberately on spaces or letters; where it appears it is almost always an alignment artifact, except to remove a space before punctuation (see 6.) or when marking ATR Noise (see 9.). If a token is genuinely split across a page or column break, use the Gap toggle instead of merging text across pages.

TEI Equivalent: Insertions appear inline with no standoff record. A Gap renders as a nested <gap/> inside the token at the page/column boundary.

<choice><orig>arriva</orig><reg>a riva</reg></choice>

3. Recognition-error correction

Rule: Fix HTR letter errors at the token level — never edit individual letters.

Details: If a letter was dropped, added, or misread (e.g. an rn/m confusion), that is bad HTR output, not a spelling phenomenon — re-annotate the whole token as Normalization, not by inserting/deleting individual characters within it.

TEI Equivalent: Produces a <span> as in (1).

4. Abbreviation resolution

Rule: Expand abbreviations to fit the word's meaning, morphosyntax, and the text's own orthographic system.

Details: Prefer whatever spelling variant is already attested elsewhere on the page over an arbitrary "standard" form. Use a Normalization annotation.

TEI Equivalent: Same as (1) — a <span> recording the abbreviated form.

<choice><orig>q̃e</orig><reg>que</reg></choice>

5. Capitalization

Rule: Normalize capitalization at sentence starts and for proper names to modern conventions.

Details: Apply this regardless of any decorative capitalization used in the source. Use Normalization.

TEI Equivalent: Same as (1).

6. Apostrophes and accents

Rule: Restore apostrophes and accents required by the target language.

Details: Add them even when they are absent from the source. Use Normalization.

TEI Equivalent: Same as (1).

<choice><orig>larive</orig><reg>l'arivé</reg></choice>

7. Punctuation spacing

Rule: Normalize the spacing around punctuation, never the punctuation itself.

Details: Remove the space before punctuation and add one after if needed. The punctuation mark is always preserved as written. Removing a space before punctuation is the one case where Deletion is a deliberate, guideline-driven choice (see 2.).

TEI Equivalent: The punctuation mark renders as <pc>; a deleted space leaves no standoff record, it is simply absent from the output.

What to preserve (do not normalize)

8. Etymological / historical spelling variants

Rule: Don't normalize genuine historical spelling variants.

Details: An f standing for an etymological ph is preserved as-is — only allographs considered the same letter in period (like ramist u/v) get normalized, see (1).

TEI Equivalent: No annotation: the text is left untouched and produces no span.

9. Numerals

Rule: Leave numerals exactly as written; tag them, don't rewrite them.

Details: No conversion between Roman, Arabic, or fractional systems, and no correction of cardinal/ordinal form or minims. It is recommended to mark up any numeral with a Markup annotation + semtag Numeral (num), even when it isn't part of a larger named entity.

TEI Equivalent: Wrapped inline in <num>, with the text itself unchanged.

10. Severe or unrecoverable ATR noise

Rule: Don't fix text that is too degraded to read confidently.

Details: Attempting to "fix" an unrecoverable span risks hallucinating content that isn't on the page. Mark it ATR Noise instead.

TEI Equivalent: Rendered inline as <unclear reason="illegible" cert="low">.

Handling uncertainty

11. Ambiguous abbreviations (names)

Rule: Don't expand a name abbreviation you can't justify from context.

Details: G. dixit in a medical text can confidently be expanded to Galenus dixit; but S. Parisiensis in a charter may give no clue whether S. stands for Stephanus, Severinus, or someone else. In that case keep the initial as written and mark it Unresolvable Abbreviation with the reason persName, orgName, placeName, or other.

TEI Equivalent: Rendered inline as <abbr type="{reason}">, with a matching subtype on the word's standoff span.

<choice><orig>.S. .B.</orig><reg>Sanctus B.</reg></choice>

Entity tagging

Rule: Tag persons, organisations, places, and numerals independently of any text change.

Details: Use Markup with the appropriate semtag (Person/ persName, Organisation/orgName, Place/placeName, Numeral/num); semtags can also be attached to a Normalization/Insertion annotation when the entity itself needed correction (e.g. an expanded name). It matters, on the long term, to keep a confidently-resolved persName (full name given, or tagged Markup once spelled out) distinct from one left as an Unresolvable Abbreviation for lack of context — that distinction is what will let us study, over time, which abbreviation patterns are reliably resolvable and which genuinely require external evidence.

TEI Equivalent: Wraps the relevant tokens inline in <persName>, <orgName>, <placeName>, or <num>.
